MAC-VO is a learning-based stereo visual odometry (VO) system whose central contribution is a \emph{metrics-aware covariance}: its FlowFormerCov frontend predicts dense optical flow together with a per-pixel 2-D covariance that is propagated through keypoint selection, outlier rejection, and pose-graph optimization. While this makes uncertainty a first-class signal inside the pipeline, the network itself remains a black box---it is unclear where the covariance comes from, whether it is trustworthy, and which conditions drive failure. We extend MAC-VO with a suite of seventeen post-hoc explainable-AI (XAI) analyses spanning gradient attribution, causal perturbation, distributional auditing, and classical-baseline consistency checks, applied across two benchmarks (TartanAir and EuRoC) and all three pipeline stages (frontend, keypoint selector, and trajectory back-end). The analyses show that the learned covariance is semantically grounded: it attends to texture and matching ambiguity, anti-correlates with scene complexity ($r=-0.305$, $p\approx10^{-4}$), remains temporally stationary, and is consistent with a learning-free stereo baseline. The covariance-aware keypoint selection induces a strong, reproducible center bias, with border rejection rates of up to $0.73$ on EuRoC---a deliberate robustness mechanism that we map and quantify as a systematic spatial blind spot. Failure conditions are dataset-dependent: motion blur is the dominant image-level predictor of pose error on real EuRoC data (Spearman $r=0.420$, $p<0.001$) yet uninformative on synthetic TartanAir imagery, and a LIME-style trajectory perturbation reveals that global accuracy hinges on a small set of currently well-estimated segments. We also identify methodological caveats---most notably that a simple motion-magnitude proxy is poorly calibrated against error, motivating the use of the learned covariance itself in future calibration studies.
